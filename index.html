Research Focus

StoryBlocks: Towards AI-Assisted Narrative Design for Data-Driven Storytelling (CHI 2026 Paper)

Yaning(Jason) Xu, Sasha Robinson, Pranav Kalsi, Swati Mishra

In this project we present StoryBlocks, an inquiry-guided interactive system for data-driven storytelling that supports writers in structuring and refining narrative designs. It utilizes Socratic Questioning technique and combinatorial optimization to assist writers with complex data exploration and personalized story construction with real-time feedback. Our system represents narratives as interconnected building blocks, and introduces a novel rendering algorithm that transforms non-linear narrative plans into linear story flows. Through a user study with novice data storytellers, the project evaluates how inquiry-guided interaction shapes writers‚Äô ideation, reflection, and narrative refinement, offering valuable insights for future work in human‚ÄìAI collaboration for narrative design.

Exploring Self-Imposed Filter Bubbles in News Recommendation (Poster)

This project explores self-imposed filter bubbles in news recommendation systems and how user behavior contributes to the reinforcement of existing beliefs. I designed and implemented a two-stage experimental platform evaluating both system-level and interface-level interventions on users‚Äô exposure to diverse viewpoints. Through longitudinal analysis and novel behavioral metrics, the project aims to assess how user agency shapes their online news consumption and evaluate the effectiveness of different interventions in mitigating such effects.


Analyzing Recommender Systems‚Äô Effects on Filter Bubbles (Paper)

This paper explores how recommender systems shape news exposure and whether they create ‚Äúfilter bubbles‚Äù - the phenomenon where excessive content provided by recommender systems reinforces users‚Äô pre-existing perspectives and beliefs. Using a collaborative filtering model and user studies, I found the system did not strongly reinforce bias: participants saw a mix of perspectives, and some even shifted their views. The findings suggest filter bubbles depend not only on algorithms but also on user choices, highlighting the need to design recommenders that balance personalization with diversity.
